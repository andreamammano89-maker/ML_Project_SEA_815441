## Section 1 — Introduction

This project analyses how Artificial Intelligence affects business workflows at
Alkemy, a digital services company. The central research question is:

> *Beyond which AI usage threshold does rework destroy profit margin?*

While AI is typically associated with faster execution, the key question is
whether efficiency gains translate into economic value — or are lost to rework,
hidden costs, and structural pricing constraints.

The dataset contains **3,248 task-level observations** with 34 variables covering
AI adoption intensity, operational efficiency, quality metrics, and financial
outcomes. Each row represents a single deliverable task within Alkemy's workflows.

The analysis proceeds in two stages: an extensive EDA phase that identifies
threshold effects and structural patterns, followed by a predictive modelling
phase that quantifies the contribution of each variable to profit margin using
both linear and non-linear methods.

## Section 2 — Methods

### 2.1 Data Cleaning

The cleaning phase was designed to preserve the structural properties of the
dataset while ensuring internal consistency.

- **Deduplication**: 48 `task_id` duplicates resolved by keeping the most
  recent record based on `updated_at`.
- **Categorical normalisation**: `team` and `task_type` standardised via
  explicit mapping dictionaries — inconsistent casing and typos reduced to
  4 team categories and 7 task types.
- **`duration_days`**: computed from `created_at` − `delivered_at`; 14 negative
  values set to NaN rather than corrected arbitrarily.
- **`legacy_ai_flag`**: `'true'` → 1, `'false'` → 0, `'unknown'` → NaN.
  Correlation with `ai_usage_pct` = −0.014 confirms they measure different constructs.

### 2.2 Missing Value Treatment

Missing values are treated as operational signals rather than purely technical
errors — they may reflect incomplete tracking or process gaps.

| Variable | N missing | Strategy | Motivation |
|---|---|---|---|
| `ai_usage_pct` | 143 | Median imputation | Distribution of missing rows matches full dataset (MCAR confirmed visually) |
| `outcome_score`, `brief_quality_score`, `sla_days`, `rework_hours` | 32–72 | Median imputation | Robust to skewed distributions |
| `billable_hours` | 81 | `hours_spent × 0.85` | Industry billing efficiency benchmark confirmed by company |
| `jira_ticket` | 331 | Dropped | Pure text identifier, no predictive value |
| `legacy_ai_flag` | 337 | Retained as NaN | Forcing imputation would introduce artificial certainty |
| `delivered_at` | 38 | Retained as NaN | Informative missingness — task not yet delivered |

To validate that `ai_usage_pct` missingness is approximately random (MCAR),
distributions of 9 key variables were compared between the full dataset and
the missing subset. The strong distributional overlap justifies median imputation
without bias.

![Missing AI Distribution Comparison](images/image2.png)

### 2.3 Outlier Treatment

No capping was applied to raw variables. The IQR × 3 rule was used as a
diagnostic tool only. Statistical outliers correspond to economically meaningful
observations: large `revenue` values are real contracts, high `hours_spent`
are complex tasks, large `rework_hours` are quality failures. Removing them
would introduce selection bias against the most informative cases.

Robustness is ensured methodologically through Spearman correlation (rank-based,
insensitive to extremes) and median-based estimators throughout the analysis.

![Spearman Correlation Heatmap](images/image3.png)

### 2.4 Feature Engineering

Derived features make the economic mechanisms explicit rather than inflating
dimensionality. All original variables are preserved; new variables decompose
their relationships into interpretable components.

| Feature | Formula | Motivation |
|---|---|---|
| `profit_margin` | `profit / revenue × 100` | Normalised margin, comparable across task sizes |
| `rework_rate` | `rework_hours / hours_spent` | Proportion of wasted time |
| `error_rate` | `errors / hours_spent` | Error density per worked hour |
| `billable_ratio` | `billable_hours / hours_spent` | Share of effort recovered as revenue |
| `rework_cost_est` | `rework_hours × median(cost_per_hour)` | Monetary cost of rework in € |
| `hidden_cost_ratio` | `rework_cost_est / cost` | Rework as share of total cost |
| `ai_usage_sq` | `ai_usage_pct²` | Quadratic term to capture non-linear AI effects |
| `ai_bucket` | `pd.cut` into 5 equal-width intervals | Threshold analysis segments |
| `budget_bucket` | Median split of `revenue` | Economic scale segmentation |
| `complexity_bucket` | Tertiles of `task_complexity_score` | Operational complexity segmentation |

All ratio-based features use a stabilised denominator (`hours_spent` clipped
at a minimum of 0.1) to prevent division-by-zero artefacts.

### 2.5 Modelling Pipeline

The modelling phase uses a unified `sklearn` Pipeline with a `ColumnTransformer`
preprocessor applied identically to all models:

- **Numeric features**: median imputation → `StandardScaler`
- **Categorical features**: most-frequent imputation → `OneHotEncoder`
  (with `handle_unknown='ignore'`)

Before fitting, variables that would cause target leakage are explicitly
excluded. `profit_margin = profit / revenue × 100`, so `profit`, `revenue`,
`cost`, and all variables algebraically derived from them (`hidden_cost_ratio`,
`rework_cost_est`, `profit_bucket`, `budget_bucket`) are removed.
Lasso cannot detect leakage silently — exclusion must be explicit and
justified before any model sees the data.

The target variable (`profit_margin`) is clipped at the 1st–99th percentile
before modelling to remove extreme estimation artefacts caused by the company's
confirmed use of estimated (rather than actual) revenue and cost figures.

## Section 3 — Experimental Design

### Experiment 1 — Threshold Detection

**Purpose**: identify the AI usage level beyond which the marginal gain in
profit margin starts to decline, and test whether this threshold varies across
pricing models and team segments.

**Baseline**: binary AI/No-AI comparison (tasks with `ai_usage_pct > 0` vs
tasks with `ai_usage_pct = 0`). This is the simplest possible comparison and
the most common approach in practice.

**Evaluation metric**: median `profit_margin` per `ai_bucket` (0–20%, 20–40%,
40–60%, 60–80%, 80–100%), with marginal effects (`profit_diff`) computed as
first differences between adjacent buckets. Median is used instead of mean
because `profit_margin` is right-skewed and mean is sensitive to the extreme
values retained in the dataset.

### Experiment 2 — Segmented Analysis

**Purpose**: test whether the aggregate AI–margin relationship is homogeneous
across structural dimensions — team, task type, and pricing model.

**Baseline**: the aggregate threshold curve from Experiment 1, treated as the
null hypothesis that the effect is uniform.

**Evaluation metric**: median `profit_margin` by segment × `ai_bucket` interaction.
Statistical significance tested where applicable. Count heatmaps accompany each
plot to flag cells with insufficient observations (n < 30).

### Experiment 3 — Predictive Modelling

**Purpose**: quantify the direct predictive contribution of `ai_usage_pct` to
`profit_margin` in a full multivariate setting, controlling for all other
observable task attributes.

**Baselines**:
- **Linear Regression**: interpretable baseline assuming additive linearity.
  Any more complex model must outperform this to justify its added complexity.
- **Lasso (LassoCV)**: same linear framework with L1 regularisation for
  automatic feature selection. The regularisation strength α is chosen via
  5-fold cross-validation on the training set.

**Primary model**: **Random Forest** with hyperparameters tuned via `GridSearchCV`
(5-fold CV on training set). Tuned parameters: `n_estimators` ∈ {100, 200, 300},
`max_depth` ∈ {4, 6, 8, None}, `min_samples_leaf` ∈ {10, 20, 40}.

**Evaluation metrics**:
- **R²** (coefficient of determination): primary metric — proportion of
  variance in `profit_margin` explained by the model.
- **MAE** (Mean Absolute Error, in percentage points): interpretable error
  in the same units as the target.
- **5-fold CV R²** on training set: out-of-sample generalisation estimate,
  more reliable than test R² alone because it averages over 5 different splits.

A **log-transformed variant** of the full pipeline is also run (`log1p` applied
to right-skewed numeric features) and compared directly to the base results to
test whether feature transformation improves predictive power.

**SHAP analysis**: `shap.TreeExplainer` is applied to the best Random Forest
to compute exact Shapley values for each feature. This gives directional,
per-observation attribution — unlike standard feature importance which only
gives aggregate magnitude.

## Section 4 — Results

### 4.1 Threshold Analysis

Median profit margin rises monotonically with AI usage intensity, from **21%**
in the 0–20% bucket to **49%** in the 80–100% bucket.

However, the aggregate trend masks critical heterogeneity:

- The binary AI/No-AI split produces the *opposite* result: AI-flagged tasks
  show lower median margins (29.1%) than No-AI tasks (42.1%). This apparent
  contradiction is resolved once AI is treated as intensity rather than binary
  flag — the 0–20% bucket, which dominates the "AI used" group numerically,
  has the lowest margins of all intensity groups.
- **Pricing model is the primary moderator**: under hourly contracts, margins
  peak at 26.3% then *decline* to 19.5% at highest AI intensity — the only
  segment where more AI leads to lower profitability. Under fixed and
  value-based pricing, margins reach 70%.

### 4.2 Model Performance

| Model | Test R² | MAE (pp) | 5-fold CV R² |
|---|---|---|---|
| Linear Regression — base | 0.2048 | 32.06 | 0.083 |
| Linear Regression — log features | 0.2487 | 30.65 | — |
| Lasso (LassoCV, α = 0.57) | ~0.20 | — | — |
| **Random Forest (tuned)** | **0.3445** | **27.64** | **0.184** |

ΔR² between Linear Regression and Random Forest = **+0.14**, confirming that
significant non-linear structure is present in the data. The threshold effects
identified in the EDA are real patterns, not descriptive artefacts.

Log transformation improves Linear Regression R² by +0.044 but leaves Random
Forest unchanged — confirming the RF already captures skewness without
explicit transformation.

|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | `pricing_model_hourly` | 15.23 | Negative — hourly pricing compresses margin |
| 2 | `billable_ratio` | 12.49 | Positive — higher billing efficiency → higher margin |

| 4 | `hours_spent` | ~8 | Negative — more hours = cost overrun on fixed contracts |
| 14 | `ai_usage_pct` | 0.70 | Weak positive — AI is not a direct margin driver |

`ai_usage_pct` ranks 14th out of all encoded features. AI usage has a
statistically detectable but modest direct contribution to margin. Its effect
is predominantly structural — mediated by pricing model, rework dynamics,
and task type.

## Section 5 — Conclusions

### Summary

The central finding of this project is that AI usage intensity does not directly
drive profit margin — it amplifies existing structural conditions. Profit margin
is primarily determined by pricing model, billing efficiency, and seniority
(structural factors outside the AI workflow), not by how much AI is used.
The only configuration where more AI systematically leads to lower profitability
is high-intensity AI under hourly pricing, where efficiency gains reduce billable
hours and therefore revenue, transferring the productivity benefit to the client
rather than capturing it as profit. The business implication is a structural
one: the intervention point is pricing reform, not AI restriction.

### Limitations and Future Work

The model explains approximately 34% of margin variance (Random Forest R²),
leaving 66% unexplained. This is not a modelling failure — it reflects the
absence of key variables from the dataset: client pricing power and tenure,
competitive context at time of sale, team capacity at assignment, and AI tool
quality (we know *how much* AI was used, not *how well*). The most valuable
next step for Alkemy would be collecting these variables, which would
substantially improve predictive power and allow causal identification of
the AI → margin mechanism.

A second open question is the SEO team anomaly: margins peak at the 60–80%
AI intensity bucket and then decline, a pattern not observed in any other team.
Understanding whether this reflects a workflow integration failure, a task
composition difference, or a small-sample artefact (n < 30 in the 80–100%
bucket) would require targeted data collection at the team level.

Finally, the research question asks about the threshold *beyond which rework
destroys margin*. The data suggest this threshold does not exist universally —
it depends critically on the pricing model. Future work should model this
interaction explicitly, ideally with a larger dataset that allows segment-specific
threshold estimation with sufficient statistical power.
